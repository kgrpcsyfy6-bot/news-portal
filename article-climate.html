UN Climate Report Warns of Accelerating Sea Level Rise Across Coastal Cities

By Dr. Elena Rossi · Climate & Environment Editor, News Online
13 June 202611:00 BST8 min read

PARIS — The United Nations Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change has issued its starkest warning yet on sea level rise, publishing a special report on Friday that revises global projections sharply upward and identifies more than fifty major coastal cities at risk of chronic flooding within the next three decades unless greenhouse gas emissions are cut dramatically. The report, compiled by 234 scientists from 67 countries and drawing on new satellite observations and ice sheet modelling, concludes that global mean sea level could rise by as much as 1.2 metres by 2100 under current emissions trajectories — nearly double the upper estimate from the panel's previous assessment in 2021.

The acceleration is primarily driven by what glaciologists describe as "unstoppable but variable" ice loss from the Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets. New data from the European Space Agency's CryoSat-3 mission and NASA's ICESat-3 have revealed that the grounding lines of several major Antarctic glaciers are retreating at rates up to three times faster than previously measured, exposing thicker inland ice to warming ocean waters.

"We are watching the world's largest ice reservoirs begin to structurally weaken in real time," said Dr Ingrid Olaussen, a glaciologist at the Norwegian Polar Institute and a lead author of the report. "What we once considered a slow, distant threat is now unfolding on a timescale measured in decades rather than centuries."

The report singles out several cities as acutely vulnerable. Shanghai, with a population of over 29 million people and critical infrastructure built on low-lying alluvial plain in the Yangtze Delta, faces what the report calls a "high confidence" risk of routine storm-surge inundation by 2050. Mumbai, where millions live in informal settlements on land reclaimed from the Arabian Sea, is listed alongside Miami, whose porous limestone geology renders traditional sea walls largely ineffective against groundwater rise. Jakarta — already sinking at rates of up to 25 centimetres per year in some districts due to groundwater extraction — appears in the report as a case study of compound risk, where sea level rise combined with land subsidence could render much of the city uninhabitable well before the end of the century.

The economic implications are staggering. The report estimates that the total value of assets exposed to a one-metre sea level rise exceeds US$14 trillion globally, with Asia accounting for more than half of that figure. Port infrastructure, which handles over 80% of global trade by volume, is particularly vulnerable: the ports of Shanghai, Rotterdam, Houston, and Singapore all lie within zones projected to experience flooding at least annually by 2070 under the report's intermediate scenario. Supply chain disruptions from port closures, the authors note, would cascade through the global economy within days.

"
Every tonne of carbon we do not emit is a cubic metre of water we do not add to the oceans.

— Amina Al Khaja, UN Secretary-General

UN Secretary-General Amina Al Khaja responded to the report by calling for an emergency session of the General Assembly on climate adaptation financing. "This is not a distant problem for future generations to solve," she said in a video address from UN headquarters. "This is a present danger that demands immediate investment in coastal defences, managed retreat where necessary, and, above all, accelerated decarbonisation." The Secretary-General urged wealthy nations to fulfil the long-delayed commitment of US$100 billion annually in climate finance for developing countries.

Climate scientists who were not involved in the report's authorship broadly endorsed its methodology while noting the inherent uncertainty in long-range ice sheet projections. Professor Kenji Matsumoto of the University of Tokyo described the report as "rigorous and appropriately cautious," but warned that even these elevated projections may prove conservative if certain tipping points — such as the collapse of the Thwaites Glacier ice shelf in West Antarctica — are triggered sooner than modelled. "The ice sheets do not care about our confidence intervals," he said. "They respond to physics, and the physics is increasingly telling us that we have underestimated the pace of change."

Governments are being urged to revise their national adaptation plans within twelve months, and the report provides a framework for city-level vulnerability assessments that account for local subsidence rates, tidal ranges, and storm surge frequencies. For the millions of people living in the shadow of this projection — from street vendors in the flood-prone wards of Mumbai to homeowners in the hurricane corridors of the American Gulf Coast — the report offers a sobering message: the water is rising faster than we thought, and the time to prepare is running short.
